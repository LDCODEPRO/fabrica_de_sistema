# LEARNING NOTES — ORCHESTRATOR AGENT

## Note 1: The Difference Between Velocity and Throughput

Velocity (story points per sprint) is team-specific and non-comparable across teams. Throughput (items completed per week) is more objective and scales better as a flow metric.  
**Lesson**: Use velocity for sprint planning and forecasting within a team. Use throughput for reporting to stakeholders and comparing delivery rates across time periods.  
**Source**: David J. Anderson, *Kanban: Successful Evolutionary Change*, Chapter 7

---

## Note 2: WIP Limits Work Against Intuition

Most people's first instinct when behind is to start more work. Kanban's counterintuitive lesson: starting more work while at or over WIP limits makes everyone slower, not faster.  
**Lesson**: When overwhelmed, finish something — don't start something. Focus over multitasking.  
**Source**: Jim Benson & Tonianne DeMaria Barry, *Personal Kanban*, Chapter 3

---

## Note 3: The Sprint Goal Is Not a Task List

Many Scrum teams treat the Sprint Goal as a label for the selected stories. This misses its purpose. A well-formed Sprint Goal is a single business outcome that the Sprint should achieve, even if individual stories change.  
**Lesson**: Write the Sprint Goal as a testable business outcome, e.g.: "By Friday, customers can reset their password without contacting support." Then select stories that serve it.  
**Source**: Jeff Sutherland, *Scrum: The Art of Doing Twice the Work*, Chapter 4; Scrum Guide 2020

---

## Note 4: Policies Must Be Visible, Not Just Known

In many teams, "everyone knows" how work moves through the board. But this implicit knowledge creates inconsistency when people leave, join, or disagree. Anderson's principle: policies must be written and posted at the point where they apply.  
**Lesson**: Add policy cards or column headers that state explicitly: "Work can enter this column only when X, Y, Z are true."  
**Source**: David J. Anderson, *Kanban: Successful Evolutionary Change*, Chapter 5

---

## Note 5: Retrospectives Fail Without Accountability

Retrospectives that generate 10 improvement ideas and implement zero are worse than no retrospective — they breed cynicism.  
**Lesson**: Limit retrospective actions to the one or two most impactful items. Assign each to a named person. Review them first at the next retro.  
**Source**: Henrik Kniberg, *Scrum and XP from the Trenches*, Chapter 9

---

## Note 6: Lead Time Includes Wait Time

Teams often focus on active work time (cycle time) and are surprised by high lead times. The gap is almost always wait time between stages.  
**Lesson**: Map the value stream and measure average wait time at each handoff. Even a 50% reduction in wait time can halve lead time without anyone working faster.  
**Source**: Lean Software Development (Poppendieck); *Toyota Production System* (Ohno)

---

## Note 7: Estimation Is a Conversation, Not a Calculation

Story points are not a measure of hours. They are a team's shared understanding of relative complexity. The value is not in the number — it is in the discussion that produces it.  
**Lesson**: If the team agrees instantly on a story estimate, it may mean the story is too small to be worth planning, or that nobody actually read it.  
**Source**: Mike Cohn, *Agile Estimating and Planning*, Chapter 4

---

## Note 8: Classes of Service Reduce Firefighting

Without explicit classes of service, every urgent request becomes an exception that derails the entire board. Defining classes (expedite, fixed date, standard, intangible) ahead of time gives the team a policy for handling urgency without chaos.  
**Lesson**: Define classes of service in the first week of using Kanban. Review and adjust after the first month of data.  
**Source**: David J. Anderson, *Kanban: Successful Evolutionary Change*, Chapter 9
